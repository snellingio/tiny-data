# Actium

Actium, Battle of Ac·ti·um, Bat·tle of | ˌbadl əv ˈaktēəm | a naval battle which took place in 31 BC off the promontory of Actium in western Greece, in the course of which Octavian defeated Mark Antony.

# activate

activate ac·ti·vate | ˈaktəˌvāt | verb [with object] 1 make (something) active or operative: fumes from cooking are enough to activate the alarm. • convert (a substance, molecule, etc.) into a reactive form: (activated as adjective) : activated chlorine. • make (a substance) radioactive. 2 US English call (a reserve military unit) into active service: the governor has finally activated and deployed the National Guard. • reinstate (a sports player) as an active player on their team, especially following an injury or suspension: the veteran infielder might be activated prior to Monday's series opener | I fully expect he will be a top-20 running back the moment he's activated.

# activator

activator ac·ti·va·tor | ˈaktəˌvādər | noun a substance that stimulates or initiates a chemical process: a chemical that serves as a bleach activator in detergents.

# active

Often contrasted with dormant, or extinct: the volcano became active on March 28 last year | Etna is Europe's highest and most active volcano. • (of a disease) in which the symptoms are manifest; not in remission or latent: active colitis. • having a chemical or biological effect on something: 350 active ingredients have been banned from pesticides. 4 Grammar relating to or denoting the voice that attributes the action of a verb to the person or thing from which it logically proceeds (e.g., of the verbs in guns kill and we saw him). The opposite of passive noun Grammar an active form of a verb. • (the active) the active voice. DERIVATIVES activeness | ˈaktəvnəs | noun ORIGIN Middle English (in the sense ‘preferring action to contemplation’): from Latin activus, from act- ‘done’, from the verb agere.

# activity
