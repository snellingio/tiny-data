# aggrieved

aggrieved ag·grieved | əˈɡrēvd | adjective feeling resentment at having been unfairly treated: they were aggrieved at the outcome | she did not see herself as the aggrieved party. DERIVATIVES aggrievedly | əˈɡrēvidlē, -vidlē | adverb ORIGIN Middle English (in the sense ‘distressed’): past participle of aggrieve, from Old French agrever ‘make heavier’, based on Latin aggravare (see aggravate).

# aghast

aghast a·ghast | əˈɡast | adjective [predicative] filled with horror or shock: when the news came out they were aghast. ORIGIN late Middle English: past participle of the obsolete verb agast, gast ‘frighten’, from Old English gǣsten. The spelling with gh (originally Scots) became general by about 1700, probably influenced by ghost; compare with ghastly.

# agile

agile ag·ile | ˈajəl | adjective 1 able to move quickly and easily: Ruth was remarkably agile and light on her feet | the changes were needed for the company to become more agile and move faster figurative. • able to think and understand quickly: his vague manner concealed an agile mind. 2 relating to or denoting a method of project management, used especially for software development, that is characterized by the division of tasks into short phases of work and frequent reassessment and adaptation of plans. Contrasted with waterfall (adjective): agile methods replace high-level design with frequent redesign. DERIVATIVES agilely | ˈajə(l)lē | adverb ORIGIN late Middle English: via French from Latin agilis, from agere ‘do’.

# aging

aging ag·ing | ˈājiNG | (British English ageing) noun the process of growing old: the external signs of aging | [as modifier] : the aging process. • the process of change in the properties of a material occurring over a period, either spontaneously or through deliberate action: the judicious use of oak aging means the wines are capable of being confused with the great French Chardonnays. adjective (of a person) growing old; elderly: an aging population | looking after aging relatives. • (of a thing) reaching the end of useful life; obsolescent: the world's aging fleet of oil tankers.

# agio

agio | ˈajēō | noun (plural agios) the percentage charged on the exchange of one currency, or one form of money, into another that is more valuable. ORIGIN late 17th century: from Italian agio, aggio, literally ‘ease, convenience’.
