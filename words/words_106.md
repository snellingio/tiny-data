# afterdamp

afterdamp af·ter·damp | ˈaftərˌdamp | noun choking gas, rich in carbon monoxide, left after an explosion of firedamp in a mine.

# afterdeck

afterdeck af·ter·deck | ˈaftərˌdek | noun an open deck toward the stern of a ship.

# aftereffect

aftereffect af·ter·ef·fect | ˈaftərəˌfek(t) | noun an effect that follows after the primary action of something: he was suffering the aftereffects of the drug.

# afterglow

afterglow af·ter·glow | ˈaftərˌɡlō | noun [in singular] light or radiance remaining in the sky after the sun has set. • good feelings remaining after a pleasurable or successful experience: basking in the afterglow of victory.

# afterimage

afterimage af·ter·im·age | ˈaftərˌimij | noun an impression of a vivid sensation (especially a visual image) retained after the stimulus has ceased.

# afterlife

afterlife af·ter·life | ˈaftərˌlīf | noun (plural afterlives | ˈaftərˌlīvz |) 1 [in singular] (in some religions) life after death: most Christians believe in an afterlife. 2 the later part of a person's life: they spent much of their afterlife trying to forget the fire | the script chronicles the afterlives of the four principal characters.

# aftermath

aftermath af·ter·math | ˈaftərˌmaTH | noun 1 the consequences or aftereffects of a significant unpleasant event: food prices soared in the aftermath of the drought. 2 Farming new grass growing after mowing or harvest. ORIGIN late 15th century (in aftermath (sense 2 of the noun)): from after (as an adjective) + dialect math ‘mowing’, of Germanic origin; related to German Mahd.

# aftermost

aftermost af·ter·most | ˈaftərˌmōst | adjective [attributive] nearest the stern of a ship or tail of an aircraft. ORIGIN early 17th century (in the sense ‘furthest back’): from after (as an adjective) + -most.
