# agrarianism

agrarian a·grar·i·an | əˈɡrerēən | adjective relating to cultivated land or the cultivation of land: Brazil is rapidly diversifying its agrarian economy. • relating to landed property: the agrarian reforms. noun a person who advocates a redistribution of landed property, especially as part of a social movement. DERIVATIVES agrarianism | əˈɡrerēəˌnizəm | noun ORIGIN early 17th century (originally denoting a Roman law for the division of conquered lands): from Latin agrarius, from ager, agr- ‘field’.

# agree

agree a·gree | əˈɡrē | verb (agrees | əˈɡrēz |) (, agreeing | əˈɡrēiNG |) (, agreed | əˈɡrēd |) [no object] 1 have the same opinion about something; concur: I completely agree with your recent editorial | we both agreed on issues such as tougher penalties for criminals | [with clause] : everybody agrees that jobs will go | [with direct speech] : “Yes, it's dreadful, isn't it,” she agreed. • (agree with) approve of (something) with regard to its moral correctness: do you agree with capital punishment?. 2 (agree to or to do something) consent to do something that has been suggested by another person: she had agreed to go and see a movie with him. • [with object] reach agreement about (something), typically after a period of negotiation: the government has agreed a plan on the country's territorial waters | [no object] : the commission agreed on a proposal to limit imports. 3 (agree with) be consistent with: your body language does not agree with what you are saying. • Grammar have the same number, gender, case, or person as: the writer made the verb agree with the subject. 4 (agree with) [usually with negative] (of food, conditions, etc.) be healthy or appropriate for (someone): she's eaten something that didn't agree with her | in Australia the climate did not agree with me. USAGE Note the distinction between agreeing to something like a plan, scheme, or project and agreeing with somebody: ‘I agree to the repayment schedule suggested; Danielle agrees with Eric that we should all go hiking on Saturday; humid weather does not agree with me’. The construction agree with is also used regarding two things that go together: ‘that story does not agree with the facts; the verb must agree with the noun in person and number’ ORIGIN late Middle English: from Old French agreer, based on Latin ad- ‘to’ + gratus ‘pleasing’.

# agreeable

agreeable a·gree·a·ble | əˈɡrēəbəl | adjective 1 enjoyable and pleasurable; pleasant: we hope to start construction later this year, when the weather is more agreeable. • friendly and good-tempered: a cheerful and agreeable companion | agreeable people tend to be modest and less boastful about their achievements. 2 [predicative] willing to agree to something: they were agreeable to its publication. • (of a course of action) acceptable: a compromise that might be agreeable to both management and unions | we hope for a mutually agreeable solution. DERIVATIVES agreeableness | əˈɡrēəbəlnəs | noun ORIGIN late Middle English: from Old French agreable, from agreer ‘make agreeable to’ (see agree).

# agreed

# agreement

# agrestal

agrestal a·gres·tal | əˈɡrestl | adjective Botany growing wild in cultivated fields. ORIGIN mid 19th century: from Latin agrestis ‘relating to the country’ (see agrestic) + -al.

# agrestic

agrestic a·gres·tic | əˈɡrestik | adjective mainly literary relating to the country; rural or rustic. ORIGIN early 17th century: from Latin agrestis, from ager, agr- ‘field’ + -ic.
