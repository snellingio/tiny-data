# accuracy

accuracy ac·cu·ra·cy | ˈakyərəsē | noun (plural accuracies) the quality or state of being correct or precise: we have confidence in the accuracy of the statistics | she hit the ball with great accuracy. • technical the degree to which the result of a measurement, calculation, or specification conforms to the correct value or a standard. Compare with precision: the accuracy of radiocarbon dating | accuracies of 50–70%.

# accurate

accurate ac·cu·rate | ˈakyərət | adjective 1 (of information, measurements, statistics, etc.) correct in all details; exact: accurate information about the illness is essential | an accurate assessment. • (of an instrument or method) capable of giving accurate information: an accurate thermometer. • faithfully or fairly representing the truth about someone or something: the portrait is an accurate likeness of Mozart. 2 (with reference to a weapon, missile, or shot) capable of or successful in reaching the intended target: reliable, accurate rifles | a player who can deliver long accurate passes. ORIGIN late 16th century: from Latin accuratus ‘done with care’, past participle of accurare, from ad- ‘towards’ + cura ‘care’.

# accurately

accurately ac·cu·rate·ly | ˈakyərətlē | adverb 1 in a way that is correct in all details; exactly: we are simply unable to predict long-term trends accurately | I have seen many meters that are not accurately calibrated. 2 so as to be successful in reaching the intended target when shooting or firing: the soldiers must be able to shoot accurately from any position at any target day and night.

# accurse

accurse | əˈkərs | verb [with object] archaic put a curse on; curse: we accurse them by the authority of the court of Rome. ORIGIN Middle English: from a- (expressing intensity) + curse.

# accursed

accursed ac·curs·ed | əˈkərsəd, əˈkərst | adjective literary 1 under a curse: the Angel of Death walks this accursed house. 2 [attributive] used to express strong dislike of or anger toward someone or something: those accursed books!. ORIGIN Middle English: past participle of accurse.

# accusation

accusation ac·cu·sa·tion | ˌakyəˈzāSHən | noun a charge or claim that someone has done something illegal or wrong: accusations of bribery. • the action or process of accusing someone: there was accusation in Brian's voice. ORIGIN late Middle English: from Old French, from Latin accusatio(n-), from accusare ‘call to account’ (see accuse).

# accusative

accusative ac·cu·sa·tive | əˈkyo͞ozədiv | Grammar adjective relating to or denoting a case of nouns, pronouns, and adjectives that expresses the object of an action or the goal of motion: the accusative case marks the object of the verb | the ending for the accusative singular. noun a word in the accusative case. • (the accusative) the accusative case: he had forgotten that “ad” takes the accusative. ORIGIN late Middle English: from Latin (casus) accusativus, literally ‘relating to an accusation or (legal) case’, translating Greek (ptōsis) aitiatikē ‘(the case) showing cause’.

# accusatorial

accusatorial ac·cu·sa·to·ri·al | əˌkyo͞ozəˈtôrēəl | adjective 1 Law (of a trial or legal procedure) involving accusation by a prosecutor and a verdict reached by an impartial judge or jury. Often contrasted with inquisitorial 2 accusatory or reproachful: he kept pointing his finger at me in a very accusatorial manner | the tone of the conversation was accusatorial and frank. ORIGIN late 18th century: from Latin accusatorius ‘accusatory’, from the verb accusare (see accuse) + -al.

# accusatory

accusatory ac·cu·sa·to·ry | əˈkyo͞ozəˌtôrē | adjective indicating or suggesting that one believes a person has done something wrong: he pointed an accusatory finger in her direction.
