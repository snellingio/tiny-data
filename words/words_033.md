# accessibly

accessible ac·ces·si·ble | əkˈsesəb(ə)l | adjective 1 (of a place) able to be reached or entered: the town is accessible by bus | this room is not accessible to elderly people. • able to be easily obtained or used: making learning opportunities more accessible to adults. 2 able to be reached, entered, or used by people who have a disability: both studios will offer private sleeping spaces and be fully accessible | features such as nonslip floors and accessible entrances | websites that are accessible to people with vision, hearing, learning, and other disabilities. 3 easily understood or appreciated: an accessible account of his theories | his Latin grammar is lucid and accessible | the story is told in a style which will be accessible to any reader. 4 (of a person, typically one in a position of authority or importance) friendly and easy to talk to; approachable: he is more accessible than most tycoons. DERIVATIVES accessibly | əkˈsesəblē, akˈsesəblē | adverb ORIGIN late Middle English: from late Latin accessibilis, from Latin access- ‘approached’, from the verb accedere (see accede).

# accession

accession ac·ces·sion | əkˈseSHən | noun 1 the attainment or acquisition of a position of rank or power, typically that of monarch or president: the Queen's accession to the throne | lost the vote on the Fortas accession to the chief justiceship. • the action or process of formally joining or being accepted by an association, institution, or group: the accession of Spain and Portugal into the European Community. • the formal acceptance of a treaty or agreement: accession to the Treaty of Paris. 2 a new item added to an existing collection of books, paintings, or artifacts: the day-to-day work of cataloguing new accessions | [as modifier] : the accession number. • an amount added to an existing quantity of something: did not anticipate any further accession of wealth from the man's estate. (usually be accessioned) verb [with object] record the addition of (a new item) to a library, museum, or other collection: each book must be accessioned and the data entered into the computer. ORIGIN late 16th century (in the general sense ‘something added’): from Latin accessio(n-), from the verb accedere ‘approach, come to’ (see accede).

# accessory

accessory ac·ces·so·ry | əkˈses(ə)rē | (also accessary) noun (plural accessories) 1 a thing which can be added to something else in order to make it more useful, versatile, or attractive: a range of bathroom accessories. • a small article or item of clothing carried or worn to complement a garment or outfit: among the hottest items are hair accessories such as rhinestone-studded barrettes. 2 Law someone who gives assistance to the perpetrator of a crime, without directly committing it, sometimes without being present: she was charged as an accessory to murder. adjective [attributive] mainly technical contributing to or aiding an activity or process in a minor way; subsidiary or supplementary: functionally the maxillae are a pair of accessory jaws. PHRASES accessory after the fact Law dated a person who knowingly aids someone who has committed a crime. accessory before the fact Law dated a person who incites or assists someone to commit a crime. ORIGIN late Middle English: from medieval Latin accessorius ‘additional thing’, from Latin access- ‘increased’, from the verb accedere (see accede).

# accident
