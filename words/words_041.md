# accuse

accuse ac·cuse | əˈkyo͞oz | verb [with object] charge (someone) with an offense or crime: he was accused of murdering his wife's lover. • claim that (someone) has done something wrong: he was accused of favoritism. ORIGIN Old English: from Old French acuser, and its source, Latin accusare ‘blame, charge with a crime’, from ad- ‘towards’ + causa ‘reason, motive, lawsuit’.

# accused

accused ac·cused | əˈkyo͞ozd | (the accused) noun a person or group of people who are charged with or on trial for a crime: the accused was ordered to stand trial on a number of charges.

# accuser

accuser ac·cus·er | əˈkyo͞ozər | noun a person who claims that someone has committed an offense or done something wrong: I dislike any law that puts the burden of proof on the accused rather than the accuser | Hero faints before her three accusers leave.

# accustom

accustom ac·cus·tom | əˈkəstəm | verb [with object] make (someone or something) accept something as normal or usual: I accustomed my eyes to the lenses | they tried to accustom him to their lighthearted ways. • (be accustomed to) be used to: my eyes gradually became accustomed to the darkness. ORIGIN late Middle English: from Old French acostumer, from a- (from Latin ad ‘to, at’) + costume ‘custom’.

# accustomed

accustomed ac·cus·tomed | əˈkəstəmd | adjective [attributive] customary; usual: his accustomed route.

# ace

ace 1 | ās | noun 1 a playing card with a single spot on it, ranked as the highest card in its suit in most card games: the ace of diamonds | life had started dealing him aces again figurative. 2 informal a person who excels at a particular sport or other activity: a motorcycle ace. • a pilot who has shot down many enemy aircraft, especially in World War I or World War II. 3 (in tennis and similar games) a service that an opponent is unable to touch and thus wins a point: Nadal banged down eight aces in the set. • Golf informal a hole in one: his hole in one at the 15th was Senior's second ace as a professional. adjective informal very good: an ace swimmer. verb [with object] informal (in tennis and similar games) serve an ace against (an opponent). • Golf score an ace on (a hole) or with (a shot). • North American English get an A or its equivalent in (a test or exam): I aced my grammar test. • (ace someone out) outdo someone in a competitive situation: the magazine won an award, acing out its rivals | it wasn't our intention to ace Phil out of a job. PHRASES an ace up one's sleeve (North American English an ace in the hole) a plan or piece of information kept secret until it becomes necessary to use it: the prime minister has several other aces up his sleeve | his formidable lead guitar skills have always been his ace in the hole. hold all the aces have all the advantages: he held all the aces: he was the Director, he could lecture on whomever he liked. play one's ace use one's best resource: deciding to play her ace, Emily showed the letter to Vic. within an ace of British English very close to: they came within an ace of death. ORIGIN Middle English (denoting the ‘one’ on dice): via Old French from Latin as ‘unity, a unit’.

# acedia
