# actuary

actuary ac·tu·ar·y | ˈak(t)SHəˌwerē | noun (plural actuaries) a person who compiles and analyzes statistics and uses them to calculate insurance risks and premiums. ORIGIN mid 16th century (originally denoting a clerk or registrar of a court): from Latin actuarius ‘bookkeeper’, from actus (see act). The current sense dates from the mid 19th century.

# acupressure

acupressure ac·u·pres·sure | ˈakyəˌpreSHər, ˈakyo͞oˌpreSHər | noun 1 another term for shiatsu 2 historical a method of controlling bleeding involving the use of a needle to apply pressure to a ruptured blood vessel. ORIGIN mid 19th century (in acupressure (sense 2 of the noun)): from acu- (in acupuncture) + pressure; in acupressure (sense 1 of the noun), which dates from the 1970s, the word was chosen to reflect the close association of the therapy with acupuncture.

# acupuncture

acupuncture ac·u·punc·ture | ˈakyo͞oˌpəNGk(t)SHər | noun a form of complementary medicine that involves pricking the skin or tissues with needles, used to alleviate pain and to treat various physical, mental, and emotional conditions. Originating in ancient China, acupuncture is now widely practiced in the West: he was willing to try anything for relief, including physical therapy and acupuncture | [as modifier] : regular acupuncture treatments were an important tool to help keep my symptoms tolerable. ORIGIN late 17th century: from Latin acu ‘with a needle’ + puncture.
