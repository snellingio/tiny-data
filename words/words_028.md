# Acadia

Acadia A·ca·di·a | əˈkādēə | a former French colony established in 1604 in the territory that now includes Nova Scotia, New Brunswick, and Prince Edward Island in Canada. Contested by France and Britain, it was ceded to Britain in 1763, and many French Acadians were deported to other parts of North America. Many later settled in Louisiana. ORIGIN from Acadie, the French name for Nova Scotia.

# Acadian

Acadian A·ca·di·an | əˈkādēən | mainly historical adjective relating to the former French colony of Acadia in eastern Canada. noun a native or inhabitant of Acadia. • mainly Canadian English a French-speaking descendant of the early French settlers in Acadia. • US English a descendant of the Acadians who settled in Louisiana in the 18th century; a Cajun.

# acanthus

acanthus a·can·thus | əˈkanTHəs | noun a herbaceous plant or shrub with bold flower spikes and spiny decorative leaves, native to Mediterranean regions. Genus Acanthus, family Acanthaceae: many species • Architecture a conventionalized representation of an acanthus leaf, used especially as a decoration for Corinthian column capitals. ORIGIN mid 16th century: via Latin from Greek akanthos, from akantha ‘thorn’, from akē ‘sharp point’.
