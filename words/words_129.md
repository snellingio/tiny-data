# akee

akee a·kee | ˈakē, aˈkē | (also ackee) noun a tropical tree that is cultivated for its fruit. Native to West Africa, it has been introduced into the West Indies and elsewhere. Blighia sapida, family Sapindaceae • the fruit of the akee, widely eaten as a vegetable, but which can be poisonous unless cooked: a tree full of akees | [mass noun] : a dish of akee. ORIGIN late 18th century: from Kru ākee.

# akimbo

akimbo a·kim·bo | əˈkimbō | adverb with hands on the hips and elbows turned outward: she stood with arms akimbo, frowning at the small boy. • (with reference to limbs) flung out widely or haphazardly: he collapsed on the bed, legs akimbo. ORIGIN late Middle English: from in kenebowe in Middle English, kenebowe possibly having the meaning ‘jug handle’ or ‘pot handle’, with the idea that the stance of a person with hands on hips resembles a jug or pot with two handles.

# Akkad

Akkad Ak·kad | akˈad, ˈäkˌäd | the capital city that gave its name to an ancient kingdom, traditionally founded by Sargon in north central Mesopotamia. Its location is unknown.

# Akkadian

Akkadian Ak·ka·di·an | əˈkādēən | noun 1 an inhabitant of Akkad. 2 the Semitic language of Akkad. Akkadian, known from cuneiform inscriptions, is the oldest Semitic language for which records exist. It was used in Mesopotamia from about 3500 BC; two dialects, Assyrian and Babylonian, were widely spoken in the Middle East for the next 2,000 years, and the Babylonian form functioned as a lingua franca until replaced by Aramaic around the 6th century BC adjective relating to Akkad in ancient Babylonia or its people or their language.
