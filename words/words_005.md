# abdication

abdication ab·di·ca·tion | ˌabdəˈkāSHən | noun 1 an act of abdicating or renouncing the throne: Edward VIII did not marry until after his abdication. 2 failure to fulfill a responsibility or duty: we can't allow such an important cause to be undermined by such an abdication of responsibility. ORIGIN mid 16th century (in the sense ‘the action of disowning a person’): probably from Latin abdicatio(n-), from the verb abdicare (see abdicate).

# abdicator

abdicate ab·di·cate | ˈabdəˌkāt | verb 1 [no object] (of a monarch) renounce one's throne: in 1918 Kaiser Wilhelm abdicated as German emperor | [with object] : Ferdinand abdicated the throne in favor of the emperor's brother. 2 [with object] fail to fulfill or undertake (a responsibility or duty): she charged the board with abdicating its responsibilities | [no object] : the secretary of state should not abdicate from leadership on educational issues. DERIVATIVES abdicator | ˈabdəˌkādər | noun ORIGIN mid 16th century: from Latin abdicat- ‘renounced’, from the verb abdicare, from ab- ‘away, from’ + dicare ‘declare’.

# abdomen

abdomen ab·do·men | ˈabdəmən | noun the part of the body of a vertebrate containing the digestive organs; the belly. In humans and other mammals, it is bounded by the diaphragm and the pelvis: pain in the abdomen can be caused by any number of things | the physician applies gentle pressure on the abdomen to turn the baby. • Zoology the posterior part of the body of an arthropod, especially the segments of an insect's body behind the thorax: the Asian hornet has a dark brown abdomen. ORIGIN mid 16th century: from Latin.

# abduct

abduct ab·duct | əbˈdək(t) | verb [with object] 1 take (someone) away by force or deception; kidnap: the millionaire who disappeared may have been abducted. 2 Physiology (of a muscle) move (a limb or part) away from the midline of the body or from another part. The opposite of adduct1: the posterior rectus muscle, which abducts the eye. ORIGIN early 17th century (in abducted, adjective): from Latin abduct- ‘led away’, from the verb abducere, from ab- ‘away, from’ + ducere ‘to lead’.

# abductor

abductor ab·duc·tor | əbˈdəktər | noun 1 a person who takes someone away by force or deception: she endured a two-hour ordeal at the hands of her abductors. 2 Anatomy (also abductor muscle) a muscle whose contraction moves a limb or part away from the midline of the body, or from another part. Compare with adductor • any of a number of specific muscles in the hand, forearm, or foot: [followed by Latin genitive] : abductor pollicis. ORIGIN early 17th century (as a term in anatomy): modern Latin (see abduct).
