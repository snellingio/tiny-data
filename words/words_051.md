# acoustically

acoustic a·cous·tic | əˈko͞ostik | adjective [attributive] 1 relating to sound or the sense of hearing: dogs have a much greater acoustic range than humans. • (of building materials) used for soundproofing or modifying sound: acoustic tiles. • (of an explosive mine or other weapon) able to be set off by sound waves. 2 (of popular music or musical instruments) not having electrical amplification: a sad, gentle acoustic ballad | old-fashioned acoustic instruments. noun 1 (usually acoustics) the properties or qualities of a room or building that determine how sound is transmitted in it: Symphony Hall has perfect acoustics. 2 (acoustics) [treated as singular] the branch of physics concerned with the properties of sound: Tyndall lectured on acoustics. 3 a musical instrument without electrical amplification, typically a guitar: these German-made acoustics are a pleasure to play. DERIVATIVES acoustical | əˈko͞ostəkəl | adjective acoustically | əˈko͞ostək(ə)lē | adverb ORIGIN mid 17th century: from Greek akoustikos, from akouein ‘hear’.

# acoustician

acoustician ac·ous·ti·cian | ˌaˌko͞oˈstiSHən | noun an expert in the branch of physics concerned with the properties of sound.

# acquaint

acquaint ac·quaint | əˈkwānt | (acquaint someone with) verb [with object] make someone aware of or familiar with: new staff should be acquainted with fire exit routes | you need to acquaint yourself with the house style. • (be acquainted) be an acquaintance: I am not acquainted with any young lady of that name | I'll leave you two to get acquainted. ORIGIN Middle English: from Old French acointier ‘make known’, from late Latin accognitare, from Latin accognoscere, from ad- ‘to’ + cognoscere ‘come to know’.

# acquaintance

acquaintance ac·quaint·ance | əˈkwān(t)n(t)s | noun 1 a person's knowledge or experience of something: the students had little acquaintance with the language. • one's slight knowledge of or friendship with someone: I renewed my acquaintance with Herbert | most men of her acquaintance were in uniform now. 2 a person one knows slightly, but who is not a close friend: a wide circle of friends and acquaintances. • acquaintances considered collectively: his extensive acquaintance included Oscar Wilde and Yeats. PHRASES make the acquaintance of (also make someone's acquaintance) meet someone for the first time and become only slightly familiar: they are anxious to make your acquaintance. DERIVATIVES acquaintanceship | əˈkwānt(ə)n(t)(s)ˌSHip | noun ORIGIN Middle English (in the sense ‘mutual knowledge, being acquainted’): from Old French acointance, from acointier ‘make known’ (see acquaint).

# acquiesce

acquiesce ac·qui·esce | ˌakwēˈes | verb [no object] accept something without protest: Sara acquiesced in his decision | he ultimately acquiesces and gives her his blessing. ORIGIN early 17th century: from Latin acquiescere, from ad- ‘to, at’ + quiescere ‘to rest’.

# acquiescence

acquiescence ac·qui·es·cence | ˌakwēˈes(ə)n(t)s | noun the acceptance of something without protest: in silent acquiescence, she rose to her feet.

# acquiescent

acquiescent ac·qui·es·cent | ˌakwēˈes(ə)nt | adjective ready to accept something without protest, or to do what someone else wants: they were acquiescent and there was no overt conflict | his acquiescent mood. ORIGIN early 17th century: from Latin acquiescent- ‘remaining at rest’, from the verb acquiescere (see acquiesce).
