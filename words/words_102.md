# afforestation

afforest af·for·est | əˈfôrəst | verb [with object] convert (land) into forest, especially for commercial use: a grant paid to farmers who afforest agricultural land. DERIVATIVES afforestation | əˌfôrəsˈtāSHən | noun ORIGIN early 16th century: from medieval Latin afforestare, from ad- ‘to’ (expressing change) + foresta ‘forest’.

# affranchise

affranchise af·fran·chise | əˈfranˌCHīz | verb [with object] archaic release from servitude: I do affranchise Saccus my slave, and Euthychia and Irene my servants. ORIGIN late 15th century: from Old French afranchiss-, lengthened stem of afranchir, from a- (from Latin ad ‘to, at’) + franc ‘free’.

# affray

affray af·fray | əˈfrā | noun Law dated an instance of fighting in a public place that disturbs the peace: Lowe was charged with causing an affray | a person guilty of affray. ORIGIN Middle English (in the general sense ‘disturbance, fray’): from Anglo-Norman French afrayer ‘disturb, startle’, based on an element of Germanic origin related to Old English frithu ‘peace, safety’ (compare with German Friede ‘peace’).

# affricate

affricate af·fri·cate | ˈafrəkət | noun Phonetics a phoneme which combines a plosive with an immediately following fricative or spirant sharing the same place of articulation, e.g. ch as in chair and j as in jar. ORIGIN late 19th century: from Latin affricatus, past participle of affricare, from ad- ‘to’ + fricare ‘to rub’.

# affright

affright af·fright | əˈfrīt | archaic verb [with object] frighten (someone): ghosts could never affright her. noun fright: the deer gazed at us in affright. ORIGIN Old English afyrhtan, of Germanic origin.

# affront

affront af·front | əˈfrənt | noun an action or remark that causes outrage or offense: he took his son's desertion as a personal affront | the sackings were an affront to justice. (usually be affronted) verb [with object] offend the modesty or values of: she was affronted by his familiarity. ORIGIN Middle English (as a verb): from Old French afronter ‘to slap in the face, insult’, based on Latin ad frontem ‘to the face’.

# affronte

affronté af·fron·té | əˈfrəntā, əˈfrəntē | (affronty | əˈfrəntē |) adjective [predicative or postpositive] Heraldry (of an animal's head) facing the observer. ORIGIN mid 16th century (as affronty): French, past participle of affronter ‘to face’.
