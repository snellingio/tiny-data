# admit

admit ad·mit | ədˈmit | verb (admits) (, admitting | ədˈmidiNG |) (, admitted | ədˈmidid |) 1 [reporting verb] confess to be true or to be the case, typically with reluctance: [with clause] : the office finally admitted that several prisoners had been injured | [with direct speech] : “I am feeling pretty tired,” Jan admitted | I have to admit I was relieved when he left | [with object] : she admitted her terror of physical contact. • [with object] confess to (a crime or fault, or one's responsibility for it): he was sentenced to prison after admitting 47 charges of burglary | [no object] : he had admitted to all seven charges against him. • acknowledge (a failure or fault): after searching for an hour, she finally had to admit defeat | [no object] : he admits to having lied. 2 [with object] allow (someone) to enter a place: senior citizens are admitted free to the museum. • receive (a patient) into a hospital for treatment: she was admitted to the hospital suffering from a chest infection. • allow (a person, country, or organization) to join an organization or group: Canada was admitted to the League of Nations. • allow (someone) to share in a privilege: the doctrine held that only a chosen few were admitted to the covenant. 3 [with object] accept as valid: the courts can refuse to admit police evidence that has been illegally obtained. 4 (admit of) [no object] formal allow the possibility of: the need to inform him was too urgent to admit of further delay. ORIGIN late Middle English: from Latin admittere, from ad- ‘to’ + mittere ‘send’.

# admix

admix ad·mix | adˈmiks | verb [with object] technical mix (something) with something else: digging is an efficient way of admixing organic matter. ORIGIN early 16th century (in Scots): back-formation from the obsolete adjective ‘admixt’ (late Middle English), from Latin admixtus ‘mixed together’, past participle of admiscere, from ad- ‘to’ + miscere ‘to mix’.

# admixture

# admonish

admonish ad·mon·ish | ədˈmäniSH | verb [with object] warn or reprimand someone firmly: she admonished me for appearing at breakfast unshaven | “You mustn't say that, Shiona,” Ruth admonished her. • [with object and infinitive] advise or urge (someone) earnestly: she admonished him to drink no more than one glass of wine. • archaic warn (someone) of something to be avoided: he admonished the people against the evil of such practices. ORIGIN Middle English amonest ‘urge, exhort’, from Old French amonester, based on Latin admonere ‘urge by warning’. Later, the final -t of amonest was taken to indicate the past tense, and the present tense changed on the pattern of verbs such as abolish; the prefix became ad- in the 16th century by association with the Latin form.
