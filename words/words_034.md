# accipitrine

accipitrine ac·cip·i·trine | a(k)ˈsipəˌtrīn, a(k)ˈsipəˌtrən, a(k)ˈsipətrēn | adjective [attributive] Ornithology relating to birds of a family that includes most diurnal birds of prey other than falcons, New World vultures, and the osprey. Family Accipitridae; treated as a subfamily (Accipitrinae) in this sense when the osprey is included in this family ORIGIN early 19th century: from French, from Latin accipiter ‘bird of prey’.

# acclaim

acclaim ac·claim | əˈklām | verb [with object] praise enthusiastically and publicly: the conference was acclaimed as a considerable success | [with object and complement] : he was acclaimed a great painter. noun enthusiastic and public praise: she has won acclaim for her commitment to democracy. ORIGIN early 17th century (in the sense ‘praise, applaud’): from Latin acclamare, from ad- ‘to’ + clamare ‘to shout’. The change in the ending was due to association with claim.

# acclamation

acclamation ac·cla·ma·tion | ˌakləˈmāSHən | noun loud and enthusiastic approval, typically to welcome or honor someone or something: the tackle brought the fans to their feet in acclamation | he was again greeted by the acclamations of all present. PHRASES by acclamation 1 (of election, agreement, etc.) by overwhelming vocal approval and without ballot. 2 Canadian English (of election) by virtue of being the sole candidate. ORIGIN mid 16th century: from Latin acclamatio(n-), from acclamare ‘shout at’, later ‘shout in approval’ (see acclaim).

# acclimate

acclimate ac·cli·mate | ˈakləˌmāt | verb [no object] mainly North American English become accustomed to a new climate or to new conditions: it will take a few days to get acclimated to the altitude. • technical respond physiologically or behaviorally to a change in an environmental factor under controlled conditions. Compare with acclimatize • [with object] Botany Horticulture harden off (a plant). ORIGIN late 18th century: from French acclimater, from a- (from Latin ad ‘to, at’) + climat ‘climate’.

# acclimation

acclimation ac·cli·ma·tion | ˌakləˈmāSHən | noun mainly North American English the process or result of becoming accustomed to a new climate or to new conditions: he spent most of his rookie season documenting his acclimation to life in the NFL | plants' acclimation to changes in temperature | [as modifier] : there is going to be an acclimation period for anyone who is new.

# acclimatization

acclimatization ac·cli·ma·ti·za·tion | əˌklīmədəˈzāSHən, əˌklīməˌtīˈzāSHən | (British English also acclimatisation) noun the process or result of becoming accustomed to a new climate or to new conditions: the most important feature of acclimatization to high altitude is an increase in the rate and depth of breathing | extensive acclimatization of foreign plants had been accomplished | I'd suggest a period of acclimatization is in order for both you and the dog.

# acclimatize

acclimatize ac·cli·ma·tize | əˈklīməˌtīz | (British English also acclimatise) verb [no object] acclimate: they acclimatized themselves before ascending Everest. • technical respond physiologically or behaviorally to a change in conditions in the natural environment. Compare with acclimate • [with object] Botany Horticulture harden off (a plant). ORIGIN early 19th century: from French acclimater ‘acclimatize’ + -ize.

# accolade

accolade ac·co·lade | ˈakəˌlād | noun 1 an award or privilege granted as a special honor or as an acknowledgment of merit: the ski resort achieved the ultimate accolade of playing host to the Winter Olympics. • an expression of praise or admiration: poignant accolades and urgent testimonials of thanks. 2 a touch on a person's shoulders with a sword at the bestowing of a knighthood. ORIGIN early 17th century: from French, from Provençal acolada, literally ‘embrace around the neck (when bestowing knighthood)’, from Latin ad- ‘at, to’ + collum ‘neck’.
