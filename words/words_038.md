# accountant

accountant ac·count·ant | əˈkoun(t)ənt | noun a person whose job is to keep, inspect, and analyse financial accounts: accountants are responsible for their client's personal and professional information | she aims to become an accountant in the near future. ORIGIN Middle English: from Law French, present participle of Old French aconter (see account). The original use was as an adjective meaning ‘liable to give an account’, hence denoting a person who must do so.

# accounting

accounting ac·count·ing | əˈkoun(t)iNG | noun the action or process of keeping financial accounts: an investigation into suspected false accounting | [as modifier] : standard accounting practice.

# accouter

accoutre ac·cou·tre | əˈko͞odər | (US English accouter) verb (accoutres, accoutring, accoutred or accouters, accoutering, accoutered) [with object] clothe or equip in something noticeable or impressive: magnificently accoutred, he was led up to the high altar | he spotted the tall figure of a splendidly accoutred cavalry officer. ORIGIN mid 16th century: from French accoutrer, from Old French acoustrer, from a- (from Latin ad ‘to, at’) + cousture ‘sewing’.

# accouterment

accoutrement ac·cou·tre·ment | əˈko͞odərmənt, əˈko͞otrəmənt | (also accouterment) (usually accoutrements) noun additional items of dress or equipment, or other items carried or worn by a person or used for a particular activity: the accoutrements of religious ritual. ORIGIN mid 16th century: from French, from accoutrer ‘clothe, equip’ (see accoutre).

# accredit

accredit ac·cred·it | əˈkredət | verb (accredits) (, accrediting | əˈkredədiNG |) (, accredited | əˈkredədəd |) [with object] 1 give credit to (someone) for something: he was accredited with being one of the world's fastest sprinters. • (accredit something to) attribute an action, saying, or quality to: the discovery of distillation is usually accredited to the Arabs. 2 (of an official body) give authority or sanction to (someone or something) when recognized standards have been met: institutions that do not meet the standards will not be accredited for teacher training. 3 give official authorization for (someone, typically a diplomat or journalist) to be in a particular place or to hold a particular post: an ambassador accredited to a northern European country. DERIVATIVES accreditor noun ORIGIN early 17th century (in accredit (sense 2 of the verb)): from French accréditer, from a- (from Latin ad ‘to, at’) + crédit ‘credit’.

# accreditation

accreditation ac·cred·i·ta·tion | əˌkredəˈtāSHən | noun 1 the action or process of officially recognizing someone as having a particular status or being qualified to perform a particular activity: the accreditation of professionals | [as modifier] : a simple accreditation process. • official certification that a school or course has met standards set by external regulators: the first fully online university to receive national accreditation | the accreditation of professional training programs in Canada. 2 an acknowledgment of a person's responsibility for or achievement of something: both parties create authorship, and to make this clear I have always used joint accreditations.

# accredited

accredited ac·cred·it·ed | əˈkredədəd | adjective (of a person, organization, or course of study) officially recognized or authorized: an accredited practitioner | an accredited college.

# accrete

accrete ac·crete | əˈkrēt | verb [no object] grow by accumulation or coalescence: ice that had accreted grotesquely into stalactites. • [with object] form (a composite whole or a collection of things) by gradual accumulation: the collection of art he had accreted was to be sold. • Astronomy (with reference to matter or a body) come or bring together under the influence of gravitation: the gas will cool and then accrete to the galaxy's core. ORIGIN mid 17th century (in the sense ‘acquire gradually’): from Latin accret- ‘grown’, from the verb accrescere, from ad- ‘to’ + crescere ‘grow’.

# accretion

accretion ac·cre·tion | əˈkrēSHən | noun the process of growth or increase, typically by the gradual accumulation of additional layers or matter: the accretion of sediments in coastal mangroves | the growing accretion of his power and influence figurative. • a thing formed or added by gradual growth or increase: the city has a historic core surrounded by recent accretions | about one-third of California was built up by accretions. • Astronomy the coming together and cohesion of matter under the influence of gravitation to form larger bodies: the planets could have continued to grow by accretion from solid debris. ORIGIN early 17th century: from Latin accretio(n-), from accrescere ‘become larger’ (see accrete).

# accretive

accretive ac·cre·tive | əˈkrēdiv | adjective characterized by gradual growth or increase: despite the accretive sediment measured, there is a clear shoreline erosion | we expect the acquisition to be highly accretive. • Astronomy characterized by the coming together and cohesion of matter under the influence of gravitation to form larger bodies: the presence of a magnetic field has a significant effect on the accretive behavior of a plasma.
