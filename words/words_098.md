# affection

affection af·fec·tion | əˈfekSHən | noun 1 a gentle feeling of fondness or liking: she felt affection for the wise old lady | he won a place in her affections. • physical expressions of affection: they are lonely and they crave affection. 2 archaic the act or process of affecting or being affected. • a condition or disease: an affection of the skin. • a mental state; an emotion. DERIVATIVES affectional | əˈfekSH(ə)nəl | adjective ORIGIN Middle English: via Old French from Latin affectio(n-), from afficere ‘to influence’ (see affect2).

# affectionate

affectionate af·fec·tion·ate | əˈfekSH(ə)nət | adjective readily feeling or showing fondness or tenderness: a happy and affectionate family. • expressing fondness: an affectionate kiss. ORIGIN late 15th century (in the sense ‘disposed, inclined towards’): from French affectionné ‘beloved’ or medieval Latin affectionatus ‘devoted’, from affectio(n-), from afficere ‘to influence’ (see affect2).

# affectionately

affectionately af·fec·tion·ate·ly | əˈfekSH(ə)nətlē | adverb in a way that displays fondness or tenderness: Tammy embraced the girl and kissed the top of her head affectionately.

# affenpinscher

Affenpinscher Af·fen·pin·scher | ˈäfənˌpin(t)SHər | noun a dog of a small breed resembling a griffin. ORIGIN German, from Affe ‘monkey’ + Pinscher ‘terrier’.

# afferent

afferent af·fer·ent | ˈaf(ə)rənt | adjective Physiology conducting or conducted inward or toward something (for nerves, the central nervous system; for blood vessels, the organ supplied). The opposite of efferent noun an afferent nerve fiber or vessel. ORIGIN mid 19th century: from Latin afferent- ‘bringing towards’, from the verb afferre, from ad- ‘to’ + ferre ‘bring’.

# affiance

affiance af·fi·ance | əˈfīən(t)s | (be affianced) verb literary be engaged to marry: Ann Elliott was affianced to Col. Lewis Morris. ORIGIN late 15th century: from Old French afiancer, from afier ‘promise, entrust’, from medieval Latin affidare ‘declare on oath’, from ad- ‘towards’ + fides ‘trust’.
