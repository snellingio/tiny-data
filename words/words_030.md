# accede

accede ac·cede | akˈsēd, ə(k)ˈsēd | (usually accede to) verb [no object] formal 1 agree to a demand, request, or treaty: the authorities did not accede to the strikers' demands | I found myself with little choice but to accede. 2 assume an office or position: Elizabeth I acceded to the throne in 1558 | he acceded to the post of director in September | by now John had died and Henry III had acceded. • become a member of a community or organization: Albania acceded to the IMF in 1990. ORIGIN late Middle English (in the general sense ‘come forward, approach’): from Latin accedere, from ad- ‘to’ + cedere ‘give way, yield’.

# accelerando

accelerando ac·cel·er·an·do | äkˌseləˈrändō, äˌCHeləˈrändō | Music adverb (especially as a direction) with a gradual increase of speed: the second half of the song was always sung accelerando. adjective gradually increasing in speed: an accelerando passage leads to the proclamation. noun (plural accelerandos) (plural or accelerandi) an accelerando passage: the first movement's long accelerando. ORIGIN Italian.

# accelerant

accelerant ac·cel·er·ant | əkˈselərənt | noun a substance used to aid the spread of fire: stolen accelerants could be used as firebombs. adjective technical accelerating or causing acceleration: accelerant factors for carcinoma.

# accelerator

accelerator ac·cel·er·a·tor | əkˈseləˌrādər | noun 1 a person or thing that causes something to happen or develop more quickly: the country has been the main accelerator of global growth for a decade. • a venture or program that promotes and aids the rapid growth of selected new small businesses: an accelerator for start-up technology companies in Buenos Aires | [as modifier] : a record number of women-led start-ups are participating in the accelerator program. 2 a device, typically a pedal, that controls the speed of a vehicle's engine: he pressed the accelerator to the floor and the taxi shot forward | the bank will have its foot on the accelerator until the economy starts to recover figurative. 3 Physics an apparatus for accelerating charged particles to high velocities; a particle accelerator: electron accelerators. • Chemistry a substance that speeds up a chemical process.

# accelerometer

accelerometer ac·cel·er·om·e·ter | əkˌseləˈrämədər | noun an instrument for measuring acceleration, typically that of an automobile, ship, aircraft, or spacecraft, or that involved in the vibration of a machine, building, or other structure.

# accent

accent ac·centnoun | ˈakˌsent | 1 a distinctive mode of pronunciation of a language, especially one associated with a particular nation, locality, or social class: a strong German accent | she never mastered the French accent. 2 a distinct emphasis given to a syllable or word in speech by stress or pitch: the accent falls on the middle syllable. • a mark on a letter, typically a vowel, to indicate pitch, stress, or vowel quality: a circumflex accent. • Music an emphasis on a particular note or chord: short fortissimo accents. 3 [in singular] a special or particular emphasis: the accent is on participation. • a feature which gives a distinctive visual emphasis to something: blue woodwork and accents of red. verb [with object] | ˈakˌsent, akˈsent | emphasize (a particular feature): fabrics that accent the background colors in the room. • Music play (a note, a beat of the bar, etc.) with an accent: the quick tempo means there is less scope for accenting offbeat notes. ORIGIN Old English (denoting a mark on a letter to indicate pitch or stress): from Latin accentus ‘intonation, mark on a letter’ (from accanere ‘to sing’, from ad- ‘to’ + canere ‘to sing’), translating Greek prosōidia ‘a song sung to music, intonation’.
