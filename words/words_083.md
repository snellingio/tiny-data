# adulterator

adulterate a·dul·ter·ate | əˈdəltəˌrāt | verb [with object] render (something) poorer in quality by adding another substance, typically an inferior one: the meat was ground fine and adulterated with potato flour. DERIVATIVES adulterator | əˈdəltəˌrādər | noun ORIGIN early 16th century (as an adjective): from Latin adulterat- ‘corrupted’, from the verb adulterare.

# adulterer

adulterer a·dul·ter·er | əˈdəltərər | noun a person who commits adultery. ORIGIN early 16th century: from the obsolete verb adulter ‘commit adultery’, from Latin adulterare ‘debauch, corrupt’, replacing an earlier Middle English noun avouterer, from the obsolete verb avoutre ‘commit adultery’, from Old French avoutrer.

# adulteress

adulteress a·dul·ter·ess | əˈdəlt(ə)rəs | noun a woman who commits adultery.

# adulterine

adulterine a·dul·ter·ine | əˈdəltərən, əˈdəltəˌrīn | adjective (of a child) born as the result of an adulterous relationship. • archaic, historical illegal, unlicensed, or spurious: an adulterine castle. ORIGIN mid 16th century (in the sense ‘given to adultery’): via Anglo-Norman French and Old French from Latin adulterinus, from adulter ‘adulterer’ (see adultery) + -inus -ine1.

# adulterous

adulterous a·dul·ter·ous | əˈdəlt(ə)rəs | adjective of or involving adultery: an adulterous affair. DERIVATIVES adulterously | əˈdəlt(ə)rəslē | adverb ORIGIN mid 16th century: from the obsolete noun adulter ‘adulterer’ (see adultery) + -ous, replacing an earlier form auoutryouse (late Middle English), from Anglo-Norman French avoutere ‘adulterer’.

# adulterously

adulterous a·dul·ter·ous | əˈdəlt(ə)rəs | adjective of or involving adultery: an adulterous affair. DERIVATIVES adulterously | əˈdəlt(ə)rəslē | adverb ORIGIN mid 16th century: from the obsolete noun adulter ‘adulterer’ (see adultery) + -ous, replacing an earlier form auoutryouse (late Middle English), from Anglo-Norman French avoutere ‘adulterer’.

# adultery

adultery a·dul·ter·y | əˈdəlt(ə)rē | noun voluntary sexual intercourse between a married person and a person who is not his or her spouse: she was committing adultery with a much younger man. ORIGIN late Middle English: from the obsolete noun adulter, from Latin adulter ‘adulterer’, replacing an earlier form avoutrie, from Anglo-Norman French avouterie.

# adulthood

adulthood a·dult·hood | əˈdəltˌ(h)o͝od | noun the state or condition of being fully grown or mature: the crucial years between adolescence and adulthood | most of the larvae die, although some survive to adulthood.

# adultness

adultness | əˈdəltnəs | noun the possession of adult qualities such as emotional maturity or responsibility.

# adumbrate

adumbrate ad·um·brate | ˈadəmˌbrāt, əˈdəmˌbrāt | verb [with object] formal report or represent in outline: they have adumbrated the importance of education as a means of social transformation. • indicate faintly: the walls were not more than adumbrated by the meager light. • foreshadow or symbolize: what qualities in Christ are adumbrated by the vine?. • overshadow: her happy reminiscences were adumbrated by consciousness of something else. DERIVATIVES adumbration | ˌadəmˈbrāSHən | noun adumbrative | ˈadəmˌbrādiv, əˈdəmbrədiv, əˈdʌmbrətiv | adjective ORIGIN mid 16th century: from Latin adumbrat- ‘shaded’, from the verb adumbrare, from ad- ‘to’ (as an intensifier) + umbrare ‘cast a shadow’ (from umbra ‘shade’).
