# aberrance

aberrance ab·er·rance | ˈabərən(t)s, əˈberən(t)s | (aberrancy | ˈabərən(t)sē, əˈberən(t)sē |) noun divergence from an accepted or usual standard: poets and scientists both speak of the capacity for aberrance in the human psyche | [count noun] : the workplace protests are somewhat of an aberrance for staff working in the intensely competitive tech industry. • mainly Biology divergence from the normal type: all these results came out with no marked aberrance. ORIGIN mid 17th century: from aberrant + -ance.

# aberrant

aberrant ab·er·rant | ˈabərənt, əˈberənt | adjective not conforming to an accepted or usual standard: this somewhat aberrant behavior requires an explanation. • mainly Biology diverging from the normal type: aberrant chromosomes. DERIVATIVES aberrantly adverb ORIGIN mid 16th century: from Latin aberrant- ‘wandering away’, from the verb aberrare, from ab- ‘away, from’ + errare ‘to stray’.

# aberration

aberration ab·er·ra·tion | ˌabəˈrāSHən | noun a departure from what is normal, usual, or expected, typically one that is unwelcome: they described the outbreak of violence in the area as an aberration | I see these activities as some kind of mental aberration | [mass noun] : the decade was seen as a period of aberration in the country's progress towards a democratic society. • Biology a characteristic that deviates from the normal type: color aberrations. • Optics the failure of rays to converge at one focus because of limitations or defects in a lens or mirror. • Astronomy the apparent displacement of a celestial object from its true position, caused by the relative motion of the observer and the object. DERIVATIVES aberrational adjective ORIGIN late 16th century: from Latin aberratio(n-), from aberrare ‘to stray’ (see aberrant).

# abet

DERIVATIVES abetment | əˈbetm(ə)nt | noun ORIGIN late Middle English (in the sense ‘urge to do something good or bad’): from Old French abeter, from a- (from Latin ad ‘to, at’) + beter ‘hound, urge on’.

# abeyance

abeyance a·bey·ance | əˈbāən(t)s | noun a state of temporary disuse or suspension: matters were held in abeyance pending further inquiries. • Law the position of being without, or of waiting for, an owner or claimant. DERIVATIVES abeyant | əˈbāənt | adjective ORIGIN early 16th century (in the legal sense): from Anglo-Norman French abeiaunce ‘the state of waiting for a claimant or owner’, from abeier ‘open wide, wait for’, from a- ‘towards’ + Old French beer ‘to gape’.
