# airtight

airtight air·tight | ˈerˌtīt | adjective not allowing air to escape or pass through: an airtight box. • having no weaknesses; unassailable: Scamp had an airtight alibi.

# airway

airway air·way | ˈerˌwā | noun 1 the passage by which air reaches a person's lungs: he kept the man's airway clear and blood circulating. • a tube for supplying air to a person's lungs in an emergency. • a ventilating passage in a mine. 2 a recognized route followed by aircraft. • (Airways) used in names of airlines: British Airways.

# airwoman

airwoman air·wom·an | ˈerˌwo͝omən | noun (plural airwomen) a female pilot or member of the crew of an aircraft, especially in an air force. • a female member of the US Air Force of the lowest rank, below sergeant.

# airy

# aisle

aisle | īl | noun a passage between rows of seats in a building such as a church or theater, an airplane, or a train: the musical had the audience dancing in the aisles. • a passage between shelves of goods in a supermarket or other building: I spend much of my time at the shops, wandering through the aisles. • Architecture (in a church) a lower part parallel to and at the side of a nave, choir, or transept, from which it is divided by pillars: the tiled roof over the south aisle. # ait

ait | āt | (also eyot) noun [in place names] British English a small island in a river: Raven's Ait. ORIGIN Old English iggath, īgeth, based on īeg ‘island’ + a diminutive suffix.

# aitch

aitch | āCH | (British English also haitch | hāCH |) noun the letter H. PHRASES drop one's aitches fail to pronounce the letter h at the beginning of words, a common feature of dialect speech. ORIGIN mid 16th century: from Old French ache, perhaps from late Latin ha ka, the names of the letters h and k.
